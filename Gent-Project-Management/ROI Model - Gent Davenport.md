# ROI Model — Gent Machine
## The Growth Case for Phase 2

**Date:** March 2026
**For:** Gent Machine leadership (via MAGNET)

---

## The Situation

Gent Machine has a large fleet of Davenport screw machines — their website lists 38. Not all are running at capacity. The constraint isn't equipment — **it's skilled people.** Gent's longtime expert (Dave) has retired. The knowledge to set up, troubleshoot, and maintain Davenports at production quality lives in whatever the current team learned from Dave, plus the manuals on the shelf.

**The core question: could Gent get more Davenports running if the skills bottleneck were removed?** Even one additional machine represents significant revenue.

Phase 1 captured Dave's expertise and Gent's maintenance documentation into an AI-powered maintenance assistant. It's live on the shop floor. Machinists have used it for real troubleshooting.

**Phase 2 isn't about protecting what you have. It's about growing what you can do.**

## The Value Model: Scale, Capability, Redundancy

### 1. SCALE — Get More Machines Running

**The headline number.** Every idle Davenport is lost revenue.

| Scenario | New Machines | Annual Revenue Added | Annual Margin (est. 35%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conservative** — 1 more Davenport | +1 | $120,000 - $168,000 | **$42,000 - $59,000** |
| **Moderate** — 2 more Davenports | +2 | $240,000 - $336,000 | **$84,000 - $118,000** |

*Gent knows better than anyone how many additional machines are viable and what the demand looks like. The model only needs ONE more running to justify the investment many times over.*

Phase 2 Managed AIR cost to Gent: **~$23,400/year** (Year 1, after MAGNET markup) dropping to **~$12,500/year** ongoing (Cruise phase).

**Getting ONE more Davenport running pays for Managed AIR in the first year alone.**

What it takes to get there:
- Hire 1 trainable machinist (doesn't need to be an experienced Davenport operator)
- Pair them with the AI cockpit from day one
- Phase 2 Managed AIR expands the knowledge base to cover setup procedures, not just troubleshooting
- Flight Instructor (Aidan) coaches the onboarding and monitors the new operator's query patterns to identify knowledge gaps in real-time

### 2. CAPABILITY — Raise the Floor Across All Operators

Every machinist on the floor — experienced or junior — gets access to expert-level guidance. This doesn't just help new hires. It makes existing operators better.

| Benefit | Impact | How to Measure |
|---|---|---|
| **Junior operators troubleshoot independently** | Fewer escalations, less downtime waiting for help | Track: queries resolved without escalation |
| **Consistent quality across machines** | Fewer scrap incidents from incorrect setup or adjustment | Track: scrap rate before/after |
| **Faster changeover** | Setup procedures available instantly vs. digging through manuals | Track: changeover time |
| **Shift independence** | Every shift has equal troubleshooting capability, not dependent on who's working | Track: downtime by shift |

**The capability gap matters because Gent serves demanding industries** — automotive (IATF 16949 certified), defense, hydraulics. Quality isn't optional. The AI cockpit helps junior operators meet the same quality standard that experienced operators hit.

### 3. REDUNDANCY — Remove Single Points of Failure

With 8 machines and a small team, one person being out sick can impact production. Dave's retirement already proved what happens when the single expert leaves.

| Risk | Without System | With System |
|---|---|---|
| Experienced operator calls in sick | That machine may not run, or runs at reduced capability | Junior operator + cockpit can cover |
| Second experienced operator leaves | Back to the Dave problem | Knowledge is in the system, not just in heads |
| Vacation coverage | Limited — only certain people can run certain setups | Any trained operator + cockpit can handle standard setups |
| Customer surge / rush order | Can't add capacity because can't staff more machines | Flex up — put a cockpit-equipped operator on an idle machine |
